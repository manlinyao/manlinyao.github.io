Transforming the mobile food vendor permitting process

Case Study

Summary

According to the U.S. Chamber of Commerce Foundation’s Food Truck National Report, Austin ranked 7th overall based on three indicators: ease of obtaining permits/licenses, difficulty complying with restrictions, and cost of permitting. While food trucks generally have low startup costs, the permitting process creates high barriers to entry, which slows local business growth.

The Office of Design and Delivery (ODD) partnered with Austin Public Health (APH) to uncover barriers and pain points for both city staff and food truck owners and provide recommendations for systems and policy changes.

My role: user research, service design/process mapping workshop co-facilitator

Deliverables: research synthesis, user personas

Tools: Sketch

Challenge

APH is responsible for conducting inspections and issuing permits of over 4,000 food establishments in Austin. Although permanent establishments are required to be inspected twice a year, it is virtually impossible to inspect mobile food trucks twice a year. Thus, Environmental Health Officers (EHOs) are driving around the city and performing random field inspections, causing an inefficient allocation of resources and potentially overlooking public health risks. Thus, how might we streamline the food trucks permitting process and support both business owners and public health officials?

Solution

The final product was a new redesign of the food truck permitting guide, now an accessible and resident-friendly digital resource.

Process
Stakeholder Mapping

In our kick-off meeting with APH staff and leadership, I led a stakeholder mapping exercise to identify all potential project stakeholders, ranging from state and local government offices to commissary owners and the general public. From the stakeholder map, we were able to identify which individuals needed to be closely involved in our project, whether through 1:1 interviews, service design and process mapping workshop participation, or email communications.

User Research

To capture a range of experiences with the food truck permitting process, I recruited a diverse group of city staff and food truck owners for 1:1 in-person interviews and completed fly-on-the-wall observations at the APH permitting office to get a firsthand understanding of the space, public messaging, staff-public interactions, digital or physical tools, and the application process itself.

Interviewed: Austin Fire, Travis County Fire, five food truck owners

Observed: permit application reviews, mobile customer lobby, Travis County Fire inspection of mobile food truck

User Personas

From my research synthesis, I uncovered many barriers for food truck owners such as time, education/awareness, and finances. In addition, depending on the owner’s business experience, connections, and resources, they had a diverse set of needs, goals, and pain points.

"It should be a lot more of a partnership."

These insights led to the construction of user personas which informed service design visualizations and recommendations.

Conclusion

Equity is a necessary component of civic service design, and thus as a researcher, it’s my responsibility to interview people with different backgrounds. Everyone has a history, a personal story that informs their needs and goals, and as public servants we must contextualize that in our work.

When a process or service is broken, it places a huge burden on both residents and service providers, decreasing trust between the two parties, while increasing friction and increasing animosity. However, by listening and understanding the concerns of all stakeholders, we can, through design, bridge those gaps and make government work for all.
